1       PIV: Introduction

 

Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) is an optical method used to measure velocities and related properties in fluids. The fluid is seeded with particles which, for the purposes of PIV, are generally assumed to faithfully follow the flow dynamics. It is the motion of these seeding particles that is used to calculate velocity information.

The experimental set-up of PIV system typically consists of several sub-systems. The particles added to the flow are known as the tracer particles. These particles are illuminated in the plane of the flow. This light scattered by the tracer particles has to be recorded either on a single frame or on a sequence of frames.  After development of the photographical PIV recordings is digitized by the scanner, the output of the CCD sensor is stored in real time in the memory of the computer directly. The displacement of the particle images between the light pulses has to be determined through evaluation of these PIV recordings. Dividing this displacement with the time interval between subsequent frames gives the velocity and hence the entire velocity field of the flow can be obtained.

Fig 1.1 Experimental arrangement for particle image velocimetry in a wind tunnel

The experimental arrangement essentially consists of the following:

á             Flow channel with the fluid seeded with tracer particles

á             Laser source for illuminating the tracer particles in the flow (generally used laser- ND:Yag)

á             Light sheet optics and Imaging optics

á             Photographic recording devices (cameras)

After obtaining the photographic images, it is given for processing which is the Digital PIV evaluation.

Micro PIV is a study of fluids in micro channels or vessels having the dimensions of the order of microns. In its setup, instead of illuminating the flow field with a laser sheet, the entire flow field is illuminated inside the channel. This is because it is not possible to get a laser sheet thickness of the order of a few microns.

1.1     PIV: Challenges

PIV, though it has a large merit list, it faces some of the challenges. Particle image velocimetry methods will in general not be able to measure components along the z-axis (towards to or away from the camera). These components might not only be missed, they might also introduce interference in the data for the x/y-components. The size of the recordable flow field is limited by the size of the tracer particles. The scattered light from each particle should be in the region of 2 to 4 pixels across on the image. If too large an area is recorded, particle image size drops and peak locking will occur with loss of sub pixel resolution. Since the resulting velocity vectors are based on cross-correlating the intensity distributions over small areas of the flow, the resulting velocity field is a spatially averaged representation of the actual velocity field. This obviously has consequences for the accuracy of spatial derivatives of the velocity field, vorticity, and spatial correlation functions that are often derived from PIV velocity fields. The flow field image obtained after DPIV evaluation always contain large number of incorrect velocity vectors due to the shear, out-of-focus particles, model surface, noise of different sources, etc. which can be eradicated and corrected by some of the Validation techniques but not all of them can be corrected.

 

1.2     PIV: Application

PIV has been applied to a wide range of flow problems, varying from the flow over an aircraft wing in a wind tunnel to vortex formation in prosthetic heart valves. Though less common, PIV can also be used for quantifying the deformation and motion of solid materials and tissues that have embedded markers or are in some other way visually heterogeneous (e.g. using fluorescent speckles or particle grains).

Rudimentary PIV algorithms based on cross-correlation can be implemented in a matter of hours, while more sophisticated algorithms may require a significant investment of time. Recently Interactive Flow Studies introduced an Educational PIV (e-PIV) system that is used in teaching fluid mechanics to students studying fluid mechanics in design, engineering and science including physiology. The development of the system is funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF). The advantage of this system is that it is affordable, safe, easy to use and versatile.

2.4     Standard Cross-Correlation (SCC) Processor

This method is commonly used for the estimation of the displacement vector in DPIV using cross-correlation between images in the Fourier domain. It works on the following algorithms:

á             Initially the image pair is subdivided into small windowed section which are masked with an image masking filter which is specially designed to eliminate edge effects that leads to aliased signals.

á             Then, the windows are converted to the spectral domain to process the cross-correlation between the images by convolving the two Fourier transformed images.

á             The correlation is then inversely transformed and the location of maximum correlation is identified, which conveys the image displacement.

á             Finally to increase the accuracy and the consistency of this method, sub-pixel resolution schemes fit curves to the correlation peak, generally using a three point Gaussian estimator.

2.6     Different DPIV Evaluation Techniques

In advanced digital interrogation techniques, there are primarily four techniques for peak estimation and processing of the data. Our prime focus is to reduce the dominance of the noise fluctuation peaks over the displacement peak. Hence we use multiple passes in an iterative manner to increase the accuracy of displacement peak estimation. The speed of this multiple passes can be significantly increased by offsetting the interrogation window which increases the fraction of matched particle images to the unmatched particle images, thereby increasing the signal-to-noise ratio of the correlation peak. This further allows unnecessary correlations to be skipped.

2.6.1     Multi-Pass – DWO

In this method, the grid size is kept constant for the subsequent passes whereas the window size, resolution can be altered along with application of validation technique in between passes. The accuracy increases if the number of passes is more (however it is useless and wastage of calculation to exceed it beyond a limit of 3). After each pass the integer portion of the local velocity filed is used in Second order Discrete Window Offset (DWO) to increase the correlation strength, limiting the correlation peak search area in the last interrogation pass. The algorithm of the following method can be laid down as:

á             The displacement vector is estimated in the interrogation window with the help of correlation techniques between the pair of images.

á             The flow field obtained is sure to contain some incorrect velocity vectors which can be eliminated using validation techniques (will be discussed in the preceding section).

á             Then we use displacement estimates to adjust the interrogation window offset locally to the nearest integer. Here as discussed, the integer portion of the local velocity field in Second order Discrete Window Offset is used to significantly increase the interrogation process

á             This process is generally repeated till 3 passes until the integer offset vector converge to +/-1 pixel.

á             This process is repeated till all the grids are covered up in the entire image plane.

Fig 2.5 Multi-pass method by discrete window offsetting

However, the computation of the flow field using this method involves tedious calculations when there is oversampling of the flow field. Hence to eliminate this demerit of the multi-pass method, we may also use the multi-grid method.

2.6.2     Multi-Grid – DWO

In this method, unlike the multi-pass method, the grid size can be altered for the subsequent pass. The window size, resolution can also be altered along with application of validation technique in between passes. Now since the grid size is varied, therefore the some kind of interpolation technique has to be employed in order to interpolate the velocity field onto the new grid. The various types of velocity-field interpolation used by us are – Nearest Neighbor (an interpolating technique which interpolates by taking into account the behavior of its neighboring areas), Bilinear and Bi-cubic interpolations. After each pass, velocity field is interpolated onto the new grid, which is then used in a second order discrete window offset (DWO). The size of the final window chosen for processing greatly depends on the particle-image density. The algorithm of the Multi-grid method can be laid down as follows:

á             The displacement vector is estimated in the interrogation window with the help of correlation techniques between the pair of images.

á             The flow field obtained is sure to contain some incorrect velocity vectors which can be eliminated using validation techniques (will be discussed in the preceding section).

á             Now on these new grids after the first pass, the local velocity field is interpolated by the help of an interpolating method.

á             Similar steps are repeated as in the case of multi-pass method.

In this method computational cost is greatly reduced and moreover the validation process also becomes easier. However, the velocity estimate that we get after the passes is limited to the accuracy of the interpolating function used.

2.6.3     Multi-Grid – Deform

This is a higher order multi-grid method in which the image window is deformed to diminish the loss of information due to the in-plane motion of the particles. Like the multi-grid method, the grid size can be altered for the subsequent pass. The window size, resolution can also be altered along with application of validation technique in between passes. Here also we need to interpolate the velocity field onto the new grids by interpolating functions of our choice. After this the image interpolation comes into play. The image is then back interpolated onto the rectilinear grid with the help of different image interpolating function like the Cardinal Function with or without Blackman Filter. The major goal of this technique is that of maximizing the effective particle image pattern (PIP) matching during the cross-correlation analysis.

 

Fig 2.6 Schematic illustration of the image transformation due to linear in-plane motion

 

This technique can be of great use to study the vortices, as it enlarges the vorticity dynamic range with respect to conventional cross-correlation. Although, this method lead to high amount of accuracy and allows the measurement of the particle image displacement over a larger range of the velocity gradient with respect to the traditional correlation, this technique implements with a high computational cost since there was a high resolution associated with the dense data spacing obtained by means of a large overlap between adjacent windows. Therefore, this method should be used specially for high shear flows.

2.6.4     Multi-grid – Ensemble

In this technique, correlation takes place by multi-grid Discrete Window Offset. However, here instead of interpolating the velocity filed onto the grids (sub-pixels), the correlation is averaged over the entire data set. The sub-pixel estimator is then applied to ensemble correlations to generate the ensemble average velocity field. Thus it can be seen that this technique only provides an average velocity field and hence its accuracy can be questioned. This method is widely used for processing those data which require low correlation strength and so it can be used where the SNR value is sufficiently low. However, the prime advantage which this method has got is that it can be used to obtain very high spatial resolutions since there is increased correlation in each frame-pair

 

2.7     Validation Techniques

After automatic evaluation of PIV recordings a certain number of obviously incorrectly determined velocity vectors can usually be found by visual inspection of the raw data. In order to detect these incorrect data, the raw flow field data have to be validated. For this purpose special algorithms have been developed. This incorrect information may be due to the shear, out-of-focus particles, model surface, noise of different sources, etc. These incorrect velocity vectors can be detected from the figure as – 1) their magnitude and direction differ considerably from their surrounding neighbors, 2) they generally appear at the edges of the data field, 3) In most cases they appear as single incorrect vector

 

Fig 2.4 Raw data obtained from a Micro PIV experiment. The vectors encircled in red need validation

For the further processing of the flow field data it is very much essential to eliminate all such erroneous data. The two main techniques that we deal with is the – 1) Mean test, and 2) Median test

2.7.1     Mean test

This test checks each velocity vector individually by comparing its magnitude () with the average value over its nearest neighbors (). Typically a 3x3 neighborhood with eight nearest neighbors (n=8) is selected. The velocity vector is rejected if the absolute difference between its magnitude and the average over its neighbors is above a certain threshold (Є)

The test can be modified by applying it not only to the magnitude but also to the U and V components of the vector (by initializing a range for UMIN, UMAX and similarly for   VMIN, VMAX), or by utilizing a larger number for comparison.

2.7.2     Median test

This test or filtering is based on the algorithm wherein all neighboring velocity vectors  are ordered linearly either with respect to the magnitude of the velocity vector, or their U and V components. The central value in this order is the mean value. The velocity vector under inspection is considered valid if and only if

This median filtering technique is frequently applied in image processing to remove binary noise. Sometimes it can be considered more powerful than the Mean test.

After having validated all PIV data it is possible to fill in the missing data using, for instance, bilinear interpolation. For instance, if the data contains 5% spurious vectors, more than 80% of the data can be recovered by straight bilinear interpolation from the four valid neighboring vectors. The remaining missing data can be estimated by using some sort of weighted average of the surrounding data such as the adaptive Gaussian window technique. Since the experimental data are affected by the noise in contrast to numerical data, smoothing of data may be employed.

 

3       DPIV Evaluator: PIVadvance2

 

3.1     Introduction

For digital PIV evaluation, we use a GUI (graphical user interface)  named as PIVadvance2. Let us understand in brief how this GUI works. It contains devices, or components, that enable a user to perform interactive tasks. This GUI is created with the help of a GUIDE (graphical user interface development environment), which provides a set of tools for creating graphical user interfaces (GUIs). These tools simplify the process of laying out and programming GUIs. The GUIDE Layout Editor enables us to populate a GUI by clicking and dragging GUI components — such as buttons, text fields, sliders, axes, and so on — into the layout area. It also enables us to create menus and context menus for the GUI. When we save our GUI layout, GUIDE automatically generates an M-file that we can use to control how the GUI works. This M-file provides code to initialize the GUI and contains a framework for the GUI callbacks—the routines that execute in response to user-generated events such as a mouse click. (for further details on GUI and its programming refer to the HELP menu in MATLAB)

The DPIV evaluator (PIVadvance2) is a GUI which is designed to read a set singly exposed images and compute the velocity vector in the entire flow field by using any one of the cross-correlation techniques (SCC or RPC) depending upon the user. When a set of pair of singly-exposed images is loaded as a job into this GUI, it cross correlates the intensity peaks of the particles in the images and gives an estimate of the displacement undergone by the particle within that time interval between the two recordings. It also gives flexibility to the user to use various methods for the evaluation – multi-pass (DWO), multi-grid (DWO), multi-grid (DEFORM) and multi-grid (ENSEMBLE) depending on the type of flow we are investigating or the analysis of some specific regions of the flow (eg. Vortex region). The GUI has three important control sections – image control, write control and the main processing section which is the PIV control. The functions of all these controls will be described in dtails in the preceeding sections of this manual. There are interlinked sections like the Interpolation section which gets activated when the multi-grid (DWO), multi-grid (DEFORM) and multi-grid (ENSEMBLE) are put into use. There is a special section on validation which can be used by the user to get better estimation of velocity vector and eradicating the incorrect vectors. There is also a JOB list at the lower left corner which mentions the name of the current job we are working on. The functioning of each section of the GUI has been explained in the following sections of this manual.

3.3.2.2   Sampling

The sampling rate, sample rate, or sampling frequency defines the number of samples per second taken from a continuous signal to make a discrete signal. For time-domain signals, it is measured in hertz (Hz) and so, here we have to input its value in Hertz. The inverse of the sampling frequency is the sampling period or sampling interval, which is the time between samples. In PIV sampling of the images is done inorder to resolve the peak effectively and reject the noise signals. After sampling the image is quantized by which the image intensity is mapped onto a discrete variable that takes values from a finite set of numbers.
